CTC vs Gross Salary vs Net Salary

By Abdullah · Updated 9 June 2026 · 5 min read

One of the most common sources of confusion for employees is why the impressive number in their offer letter is so different from the amount that actually reaches their bank account. The answer lies in three related but distinct terms: CTC, gross salary, and net salary. Understanding how they differ helps you negotiate better, plan your budget, and read your payslip with confidence.

1. CTC (Cost to Company)

CTC stands for "Cost to Company" — the total amount an employer spends on you in a year. This is the big figure usually quoted in job offers. Importantly, CTC includes much more than the cash you receive. It bundles in your direct salary plus indirect benefits such as the employer's contribution to your Provident Fund, gratuity, health insurance premiums, and sometimes perks like meal cards or transport.

Because CTC includes these extra benefits, it is always higher than the salary you actually take home. Two jobs with the same CTC can offer very different take-home pay depending on how the package is structured.

2. Gross Salary

Gross salary is your total earnings before any deductions, but after removing the employer's own contributions (like its share of PF and gratuity) from the CTC. In other words, gross salary is the sum of your Basic Pay, HRA, Dearness Allowance, and all other allowances and bonuses. It is the "Total Earnings" figure you see on your salary slip.

3. Net Salary (Take-Home Pay)

Net salary, also called in-hand or take-home pay, is what remains after all deductions are subtracted from your gross salary. Deductions typically include your share of Provident Fund, Professional Tax, and TDS (income tax). This is the actual amount credited to your bank account each month.

Dividing the net annual salary by 12 gives the approximate monthly take-home pay. The exact figures vary by company structure and tax rules, but the relationship always holds: CTC > Gross Salary > Net Salary.

Key Takeaways

  • CTC is what you cost your employer — the headline offer figure.
  • Gross salary is what you earn before deductions.
  • Net salary is what you actually take home.
  • When comparing job offers, always ask for the expected net/in-hand figure, not just the CTC.
