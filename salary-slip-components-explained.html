Salary Slip Components Explained

By Abdullah · Updated 9 June 2026 · 6 min read

At first glance, a salary slip can look like a confusing list of abbreviations and numbers. But once you understand what each line means, your payslip becomes a simple, transparent story of how your salary is built and where every rupee goes. In this guide we break down the most common components found on a salary slip, grouped into two parts: earnings (money added to your salary) and deductions (money subtracted from it).

Part 1: Earnings

Earnings are the components that together make up your gross salary. The most common ones are:

1. Basic Salary

Basic salary is the core, fixed part of your pay and usually forms the largest single component — often 40% to 50% of your total salary. Many other components, such as Provident Fund and HRA, are calculated as a percentage of the basic salary, so it has a knock-on effect on your overall package. It is fully taxable.

2. House Rent Allowance (HRA)

HRA is paid to help cover the cost of rented accommodation. If you live in a rented home, a portion of your HRA may be exempt from income tax under the applicable rules, which makes it a tax-efficient component. Employees living in their own homes still receive HRA, but it is then fully taxable.

3. Dearness Allowance (DA)

Dearness Allowance is a cost-of-living adjustment most commonly paid by government and public sector employers. It is designed to offset the impact of inflation and is usually revised periodically. DA is fully taxable.

4. Conveyance & Other Allowances

Conveyance allowance covers travel between home and work, while special or other allowances are flexible amounts used to balance the total package. Employers may also offer medical allowance, meal allowance, or performance-linked incentives depending on company policy.

Gross Salary = Basic + HRA + DA + all allowances and bonuses. This is the total of your earnings before any deductions.

Part 2: Deductions

Deductions are amounts subtracted from your gross salary. Common deductions include:

1. Provident Fund (PF)

The Employees' Provident Fund is a retirement savings scheme. A fixed percentage of your basic salary is contributed by you, and your employer typically contributes a matching amount. This money grows over time and supports you after retirement, making it a form of forced — but beneficial — savings.

2. Professional Tax

Professional tax is a small tax levied by some state governments on salaried individuals. The amount is usually modest and capped at a maximum annual figure. It does not apply in every region.

3. Tax Deducted at Source (TDS)

TDS is income tax that your employer deducts directly from your salary and deposits with the tax authorities on your behalf. The amount depends on your total income and the tax slab you fall into. At the end of the financial year, this is adjusted against your actual tax liability.
